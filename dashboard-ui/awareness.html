Cybersecurity Awareness

Learn how to spot online scams before they become a risk.

Online scams often rely on urgency, fear, fake trust, and small mistakes. This page helps users recognize common threats and practice safer online habits.

View Common Questions See Safety Tips

Quick Safety Check

  • Pause before clicking unexpected links.
  • Check the sender’s email address carefully.
  • Use multi-factor authentication when available.
  • Never share passwords or verification codes.

Why cybersecurity awareness matters

Fraud detection tools can flag suspicious behavior, but users are still one of the strongest lines of defense. Recognizing scam patterns can help prevent stolen accounts, financial loss, identity theft, and unauthorized access.

Common Scam Patterns

What users should watch for

01

Phishing Emails

Attackers may pretend to be a bank, workplace, delivery service, or trusted company to trick users into clicking a link or entering login details.

02

Fake Urgency

Messages like “act now,” “your account will close,” or “payment failed” are often used to pressure users into making quick decisions.

03

Password Attacks

Weak or reused passwords make it easier for attackers to break into accounts, especially if one password is leaked from another website.

04

Social Engineering

Scammers may pretend to be a coworker, support agent, friend, or authority figure to convince users to share private information.

Safe Online Habits

Simple steps that reduce risk

1

Verify before trusting

If a message asks for sensitive information, verify it through an official website, phone number, or known contact method.

2

Use strong authentication

Use unique passwords and turn on multi-factor authentication to make accounts harder to compromise.

3

Report suspicious activity

Reporting suspicious emails, transactions, or login attempts helps security teams respond faster and prevent further damage.

FAQ

Common cybersecurity awareness questions

Phishing is when an attacker pretends to be a trusted person or organization to trick users into clicking a link, downloading a file, or sharing sensitive information.

Look for misspellings, unusual sender addresses, unexpected attachments, urgent language, or links that do not match the official website.

Do not enter any information. Close the page, change your password if needed, enable multi-factor authentication, and report the incident to the proper support or security contact.

Multi-factor authentication adds another verification step, so even if someone steals a password, they may still be blocked from accessing the account.

Social engineering is when attackers manipulate people into giving access, sharing information, sending money, or bypassing normal security steps.

Change your password, review recent activity, enable MFA, contact your bank or service provider if money is involved, and report the activity immediately.
